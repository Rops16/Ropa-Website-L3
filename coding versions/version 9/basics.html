Budgeting

A budget is a plan that helps you manage your money by tracking how much you earn, spend, and save. It basically prevents you from going broke. The main idea is to list all your expense (what you must pay for) and your income (any money that’s coming in) to have a big overview of where your money is going. The goal is to ensure your expenses aren't more than your income. Tools like budgeting apps or spreadsheets can help you stay organized.



Why is it important to budget?

  • It helps prevent overspending
  • Help you predict future spending and gain awareness of what is truly going on with your money.
  • Help reach financial goals


Budgets are the first step in reaching bigger financial goals which make it a worthwhile task to draw up one.
An easy way to get started is to use the famous 50/30/20 rule when managing your money.


  • 50% for needs (rent, food, bills)
  • 30% for wants (fun stuff)
  • 20% for savings and debt


Here are some quick and easy steps to create a simple budget:

Take note of how much money you're making - after taxes - and from where. Maybe you have a job, business, investments or just simply getting pocket money. Ensure you also note down how frequently you're paid.


Take note of all your expenses and how much they each cost and their total. Examples of some expenses are:

  • Rent / mortgage payments
  • Electricity
  • Gas
  • Water
  • Food
  • Medical Costs

To make sure you've recorded all your expenses, you can look at your bills or bank statements. A bank statement is just a document that summarises all your transactions with a bank.

Set your spending and saving limits. Once you've noted how much money you're getting in and how much is going to have to go out (expenses). You will have money remaining which can be used for your wants and future savings. Wants involve things for entertainment such as: eating out , hobbies, luxury items etc. It's important to plan on how you want to spend the remainder of your money otherwise you'll find yourself not being able to pay for an iced Americano and wondering where the rest of your money went.


If you're saving - which most should be as it's beneficial- set your savings goal. Having some savings can create a safety net for unexpected expenses. Even a small amount set aside regularly will make a difference.


Saving & Spending

Saving means setting aside money for future goals (a car, college, or emergencies). Spending is about using money wisely for things you need and want. Now, these concepts are important as they help build financial security, while smart spending prevents debt and stress.



Quick tip: Pay yourself first! Set up automatic transfers to a savings account every time you get paid. Even $20 a week adds up over time. This links with the 50-30-20 rule in budgeting.



To balance saving and spending, prioritize needs over wants and avoid impulse purchases. Automating your savings can help you stay consistent, and tracking your spending can reveal areas where you can cut back. This is where a budget can come in and help you.

Bank Accounts

A bank account is where you store your money safely. The most common types include checking (for everyday transactions) and savings (for growing your money). Having a bank account is a must for everyone because it’s used by people and businesses everywhere around the world.



Bank accounts help keep your money secure, help you track spending, and can even earn your interest. They also allow you to make quick and easy payments online and make it easy to view your spending history which can come in handy when making your budgets and just tracking your spending in general.


Some people have multiple bank accounts as they can be used for budgeting, tracking business income or sending and storing foreign currency.

Credit and Debt Management

Credit allows you to borrow money, like when you use a credit card or get a loan. If you handle credit wisely, it helps you build a good credit score. Having a good credit score makes it easier to get loans, rent a home, or even secure some jobs. Your credit score is influenced by paying bills on time, how much debt you have, and how long you've been using credit.



To manage debt effectively, only borrow what you can afford to repay and always pay on time. High-interest debts, such as credit card debt, can grow very quickly, so it's smart to pay them off first. You can tackle debt with strategies like the "debt snowball," which involves paying off smaller debts first to build momentum, or the "debt avalanche," which targets high-interest debts first to save on interest costs. These methods can help keep your debts under control.

Some things or general things to remember are:

  • Pay bills on time
  • Keep credit card balances low (under 30% of your limit).
  • Avoid unnecessary debt (like buying stuff you can't afford).
  • If you own a credit card, use it responsibly and don't spend more than what you can pay back.

Investing

Investing is a way to grow your money over time by putting it into assets like stocks, bonds, or mutual funds. Assets are things that only bring money into our account.


Things like investments, renting out property (Real Estate) and savings accounts. Stocks represent ownership in a company – like the Apple or Tesla stocks - while bonds are loans you give to a company or government in exchange for interest. Mutual funds are like a shared investment pot where many people pool their money together to buy a variety of stocks, bonds, and other investments, managed by professionals who aim to grow the money for everyone involved.


Investing is highly advantageous, especially at young age, because your money is almost guaranteed to grow (not always) over a long period of time. This comes from the concept of compound interest.


For example, if you invest $1,000 and earn a 5% return, you’ll have $1,050 after one year. The next year, you’ll earn 5% on $1,050, not just the original $1,000. Starting early and staying consistent are key to building wealth through investing.



However, investing does have a downside and that is it’s possible to lose some or all your money as it’s impossible to accurately guess the increase in value of whatever you may have invested in.

Taxes

Taxes are compulsory payments made to the government, usually based on your income, purchases, or property. Income taxes are the most common, and they fund public services like schools, roads, and healthcare. Each year, you file a tax return to report your income and expenses, which determines how much you owe or if you’ll get a refund.



It’s important to pay taxes on time to avoid penalties and interest. Many people use tax software or hire professionals to help them file accurately. Keeping records of your income and expenses throughout the year makes the process easier.


Filing a tax return is a way to report your income and the taxes you’ve paid. In New Zealand, for instance, this involves IRD (Inland Revenue Department). Filing might get you a refund if you overpaid during the year or may reveal you owe more taxes. You’ll need an IRD number (in New Zealand) or similar tax ID in other countries. It’s unique to you and used for all tax-related matters.


Not all money is taxed the same way. For instance, money earned from a job may be taxed differently from money you make from selling items online or investments.


This is not all there is to know about taxes, but it’s worth to note as it scratches the surface. Taxes are a part of life and filing them accurately and on time is crucial. Good record-keeping and planning can help you avoid surprises at tax time.

Loans

Read the fine print! Some loans come with hidden fees, like origination fees or prepayment penalties. Make sure you understand all terms before signing.



The concept of a credit score is important when it comes to borrowing. A credit score is like a report card for your financial behaviour, but instead of grades, it’s a number—typically ranging from 0 to 1,000 or 300 to 850, depending on the country. It shows how good you are at borrowing money and paying it back.

  • A high score means you’re good at managing your money, which makes lenders (like banks) more likely to trust you with loans or credit cards – essentially trust you to borrow.
  • A low score means you might have struggled to pay back borrowed money, and lenders could see you as a risk.

Your score is influenced by things like:

  • Paying bills on time.
  • How much debt you already have.
  • How long you’ve been borrowing (your credit history).

It’s a key tool that lenders use to decide if they’ll approve you for a loan or credit, and what interest rate they’ll offer. Essentially, it’s your “trustworthiness” rating in the financial world!


Before borrowing, make sure you can afford the monthly payments and understand the terms of the loan. Avoid borrowing for unnecessary expenses and always have a plan to pay it back. It’s also important to note that different types of loans have specific terms and conditions, so make sure the loan fits your needs and repayment ability. Ensure to read the fine print! Some loans come with hidden fees, like origination fees or prepayment penalties. Make sure you understand all terms before signing.



Borrowing can help you achieve big goals, but it comes with responsibility. Only borrow what you can afford to repay and compare interest rates to save money.
